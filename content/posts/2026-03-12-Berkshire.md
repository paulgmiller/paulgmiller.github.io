# Will Berkshire Hathaway Still Beat the Market After Buffett?

A question I’ve been thinking about while considering whether to exchange Berkshire Hathaway stock into a diversification fund:

> Why assign something like a **40% probability that Berkshire still outperforms the market** once Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger are gone?

Buffett and Munger were extraordinary outliers. Expecting their successors to replicate that performance seems unrealistic. But Berkshire is also not a normal company — it has structural advantages that could still generate some alpha.

Below is how I think about the question.

---

# 1. First principle: Buffett’s performance was statistically extreme

From 1965–2023 approximately:

| Metric | Berkshire | S&P 500 |
|------|------|------|
Annual return | ~19.8% | ~10.2% |
Excess return | ~9–10% |

Sustaining **~10% annual alpha for nearly 60 years** is almost unheard of in financial history.

Implication:

> Even if Berkshire’s next leaders are excellent, the **base-rate expectation is regression toward the market average**.

The real question therefore becomes:

**Does Berkshire still possess structural advantages that could generate some modest alpha even without Buffett?**

---

# 2. Greg Abel (future CEO)

## Background

Greg Abel currently runs Berkshire’s non-insurance operating businesses and previously led **Berkshire Hathaway Energy**.

Under his leadership:

- MidAmerican / Berkshire Hathaway Energy grew from a ~$2B acquisition
- to a **$100B+ enterprise**
- with major expansion into renewables and infrastructure.

## Strengths

Investors tend to view Abel as strong in three areas:

- operational discipline
- large-scale capital deployment
- internal credibility with Buffett

Buffett himself has repeatedly said:

> “Greg understands capital allocation.”

That is a remarkable endorsement coming from Buffett.

## Limitations

However, Abel is **not Buffett**.

He is:

- not a public equities investor
- not known for macro insight
- not a famous stock picker

Abel’s role will likely be more about **capital allocation across Berkshire’s businesses**, not selecting individual stocks.

Confidence: **very high**.

Both are impressive investors, but **neither has demonstrated Buffett-level results over decades with massive capital**.
